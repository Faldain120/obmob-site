OB/GYN Mock Orals: How to Practice (and What Works)

The oral boards are a speaking exam, so the only practice that transfers is speaking. Here are the ways to run mock orals, their trade-offs, and what effective practice actually looks like.

In short: a mock oral is a rehearsal of the real exam. Someone questions you on a case and you defend your reasoning out loud, without hints. The best practice is spoken, under questioning, on the kinds of cases you’ll actually face, repeated often enough that the format stops being the hard part.

What a mock oral is

A mock oral mirrors the ABOG Certifying exam: you’re given a case, an examiner asks you to work through it aloud, and they probe your reasoning (changing variables, asking why, and pushing on alternatives) without coaching you. The point isn’t to get a “right answer” on paper; it’s to build the reflex of thinking clearly and speaking confidently under pressure.

Ways to practice, and the trade-offs

Every method helps; each has a limitation. Most candidates use more than one.

MethodStrengthsLimitations
Attending or co‑residentReal mentorship, clinical nuance, honest feedbackHard to schedule; limited reps; depends on their availability
Formal mock-oral courseStructured, exam-realistic, experienced examinersExpensive; fixed dates; generic cases, not yours
Solo self-quizzingAlways available; freeNo questioning, no pressure, no outside feedback

What good practice actually looks like

  • Out loud. Speaking is the skill being tested. Silent review doesn’t build it.
  • Under questioning. The value is in defending your reasoning when someone pushes back.
  • On relevant cases. Rehearse both your own case list and structured cases for breadth.
  • Repeatable. Enough reps that the format feels routine and your nerves settle.
  • With feedback. Know specifically what was strong and what to fix, not just a vague sense.

Common mistakes

  • Reviewing silently instead of practicing out loud.
  • Only practicing what you know: avoiding your weak areas.
  • Cramming a few sessions right before the exam instead of spacing reps out.
  • Skipping your own case list: the half most likely to catch you off guard.
  • Practicing without pressure so the real room feels nothing like rehearsal.
